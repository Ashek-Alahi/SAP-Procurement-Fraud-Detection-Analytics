# Executive Findings Report — SAP Procurement Fraud Detection Analytics

## 1. Executive Summary

This project analyzed simulated SAP S/4HANA procurement data to identify high-risk procure-to-pay exceptions across vendor master data, purchase orders, invoices, and approval workflow activity.

The analytics identified **102 exceptions** with total suspected financial exposure of **¥131,616,000**. The two largest exposure areas are duplicate invoices and abnormal vendor patterns, which together represent **¥113,640,000** of suspected exposure.

| Fraud category | Exceptions found | Suspected exposure | Risk level | Management interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Duplicate invoices | 67 | ¥60,706,000 | High | Highest direct duplicate-payment risk; should be reviewed before payment or recovered if already paid. |
| Abnormal vendors | 10 | ¥52,934,000 | Medium | Highest vendor master governance risk; includes duplicate bank account and new-vendor high-payment indicators. |
| Approval bypasses | 15 | ¥10,723,000 | Medium | Indicates workflow authority gaps, missing approver master data, or inactive approver usage. |
| Split purchases | 10 | ¥7,253,000 | High | Indicates possible purchase order splitting to avoid the ¥500,000 approval threshold. |
| **Total** | **102** | **¥131,616,000** | — | Requires structured investigation and SAP control remediation. |

## 2. Business Context

Procurement fraud risk is significant because SAP procure-to-pay processes connect master data, purchase order approvals, invoice verification, goods receipt matching, and vendor payment execution. A control failure in any part of the process can result in duplicate payments, unauthorized spend, payments to risky vendors, or workflow approval violations.

This project focuses on four practical fraud analytics questions:

1. Did any vendors submit duplicate or near-duplicate invoices?
2. Were purchase orders split into smaller transactions to bypass approval limits?
3. Do vendor master and payment patterns indicate ghost-vendor or shell-vendor risk?
4. Were purchase orders approved by missing, inactive, or under-authorized approvers?

## 3. Scope and Source Data

The analysis used four synthetic SAP-style datasets:

| Dataset | SAP equivalent | Records analyzed | Business meaning |
|---|---|---:|---|
| `vendors.csv` | LFA1 / LFB1 / Business Partner vendor master | 50 | Vendor identity, bank details, payment terms, vendor activity, and ghost-vendor indicators. |
| `purchase_orders.csv` | EKKO / EKPO | 500 | Purchase order values, dates, vendors, approval activity, plants, and material groups. |
| `invoices.csv` | RBKP / RSEG | 600 | Invoice amounts, posting dates, payment status, invoice numbers, and three-way-match status. |
| `employees.csv` | Employee / workflow authorization master | 30 | Approver identity, department, approval limit, approval level, and active status. |

The fraud-label columns in the generated data are treated as validation fields. The reporting conclusions are based on detection outputs generated by the business rules in the dashboard and analysis layers.

## 7. SAP Control Themes

The findings suggest four core remediation themes:

1. **Strengthen MIRO duplicate invoice prevention** so same-vendor duplicate invoices are blocked or routed to exception review before payment.
2. **Improve vendor master governance** through dual control, duplicate bank account blocking, and enhanced new-vendor monitoring.
3. **Harden workflow approval controls** so SAP does not allow missing, inactive, or under-authorized approvers to release purchase orders.
4. **Monitor cumulative vendor spend** so same-day or short-window purchase order splitting is detected even when each individual PO is below approval threshold.

## 8. Limitations and Interpretation

Analytics exceptions are not proof of fraud. They are indicators that require business validation. False positives can occur due to legitimate recurring invoices, shared bank accounts within a corporate group, emergency approvals, or operationally valid same-day purchase orders.

Before confirming fraud, a real organization should validate each exception against:

- Supplier contracts and statements.
- Invoice images and invoice-number normalization.
- Goods receipt and service entry records.
- Purchase requisitions and release strategy logs.
- Vendor master change documents.
- Payment run history and clearing documents.
- Employee approval delegation records.

## 9. Final Business Conclusion

The project demonstrates how SAP-style procurement data can be transformed into practical fraud risk intelligence. The strongest business value is not only detecting exceptions, but connecting each exception to a control action that Finance, Procurement, Internal Audit, and SAP teams can implement.

For portfolio purposes, this final report shows the full analytics lifecycle: business risk definition, SAP data modeling, rule-based detection, dashboard communication, and control remediation planning.
